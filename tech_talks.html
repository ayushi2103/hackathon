Why we have too few women leaders

December 21, 2010 Sheryl Sandberg

Facebook COO Sheryl Sandberg looks at why a smaller percentage of women than men reach the top of their professions and offers 3 powerful pieces of advice to women aiming for the C-suite.

Throughout her talk, Sheryl Sandberg touches on the problems that women face at work and the obstacles holding them back. But it was a rousing statement that prompted applause and cheers from the female members of the audience.

“Success and likeability are positively correlated for men and negatively correlated for women,” she says, encapsulating and confirming the truth that women in the workplace have long-known to be true. Being likeable and successful is difficult, if not entirely impossible in workplace environments where the glass ceiling is so low women can only crawl towards equality.

Teach girls bravery, not perfection

March 28, 2016 Reshma Saujani

Led by the founder of Girls Who Code, Reshma Saujani discusses the societal expectations of boy and girls, and how these expectations can affect their career choices. By instilling in young girls the idea they should strive for perfection, she argues, they learn to settle for what ensures success, rather than taking the risks needed to propel women forward in work and in life.

“The difference is in how boys and girls approach a challenge. And it doesn’t just end in fifth grade,” Saujani states. “An HP report found that men will apply for a job if they meet only 60% of the qualifications, but women, women will apply only if they meet 100% of the qualifications.”

If there’s anything women need to learn from men here, it’s to believe in your own self-worth. And this is coming from someone who never wrote about software or IT management and applied to Capterra anyway. Because I believed in myself and my abilities, which was mostly due to another female professional believing in me as well.

Why do ambitious women have flat heads?

March 27, 2010 Dame Stephanie Shirley

If you’re a woman with the odds stack against you and you need some examples of female resilience, look no further than Dame Stephanie Shirley, a Jewish refugee during WWII, the founder of a software company in the early 1960’s, and the mother of an autistic child, who later passed away.

“You can always tell ambitious women by the shape of our heads,” she says. “They’re flat on top for being patted patronizingly. And we have larger feet to stand away from the kitchen sink.”

Can we all "have it all"?

March 12, 2014 Anne-Marie Slaughter

Heartbreaking and poignant, this TED Talk is sure to touch any female professional juggling and struggling with the role of business leader and parent. But as Anne-Marie Slaughter argues, when push comes to shove, family life should win out, especially since it can even help your career.

“I suggest that real equality, full equality, does not just mean valuing women on male terms,” Slaughter states. “It means creating a much wider range of equally respected choices for women and for men. And to get there, we have to change our workplaces, our policies and our culture….In the workplace, real equality means valuing family just as much as work, and understanding that the two reinforce each other.”

Slaughter takes off on a logic-based argument, presenting the notion that those who value their family before career also bring a “wider range of experiences and contacts,” since they socialize at their children’s events, a sort of a parent-based networking.
